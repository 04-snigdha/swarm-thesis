4. Post-Experiment Analysis and Methodological Refinements

4.1 Supervisor Feedback and Pre-Experiment Refinements

Before the main experiment was launched, a supervisor meeting produced several critical changes to the simulation architecture. The agents lacked a fixed home base, so I implemented a global HOME_BASE_COORD at (700, 350) to give ants a consistent return target between trips. Navigation behaviour was also broken: agents were spinning in place with no coherent direction. I resolved this by implementing a Lévy walk with heading drift and a wall-stall escape mechanism, which gave agents more biologically plausible exploratory movement. A vision radius of approximately 50 pixels was added so that ants could detect the payload without requiring exact contact, reflecting the short-range chemical detection seen in real ant colonies.

On the experimental design side, my supervisor stressed that stochastic shuffling had to be operationalised as a controllable independent variable. I formalised this as a SHUFFLE_RANDOMNESS parameter ranging from 0.0 (fully deterministic) to 1.0 (fully random repositioning), giving me a clean lever to sweep across conditions. The state machine was redesigned and documented as an 8-state FSM, and random seeds were set per trial using random.seed(trial_id) to ensure reproducibility. All parameters were documented ahead of the lock-in meeting following this session.

4.2 Main Experiment Results

The main experiment ran 5,000 trials across a full factorial design: 4 shapes (Circle, Square, L-shape, C-shape) × 5 swarm sizes [15, 20, 25, 30, 35] × 5 SR levels [0.0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0] × 50 trials per condition. The overall success rate was 64.4% (3,218 out of 5,000 trials).

Results varied substantially by shape. The Circle achieved an 88.1% success rate and the Square 83.9%; in both cases, success was driven primarily by swarm size, with SR level having a weak and inconsistent effect. The L-shape reached 62.4% overall, but with a more interesting pattern: SR = 0.0 yielded the highest success rate (74%) while SR = 1.0 was worst (52%), and success dropped at swarm size 35, suggesting that overcrowding begins to interfere with transport on asymmetric shapes. The C-shape was the most striking result, with only 23.0% success. Critically, failure rates worsened as swarm size increased beyond 25, and no SR level was able to compensate — a pattern that immediately pointed to a geometric rather than a search problem.

4.3 Failure Mode Classification

To understand why trials were failing, I used the Peak_Attached variable as a proxy to classify failure type. Trials with Peak_Attached ≤ 2 were classified as search failures, meaning agents never successfully found and attached to the payload. Trials with Peak_Attached ≥ 3 were classified as stuck failures, meaning agents attached in meaningful numbers but the collective nonetheless failed to complete transport.

Across all 1,782 failed trials, 75.1% were stuck failures rather than search failures. At swarm size 15, search failures dominated across all shapes, which is consistent with too few agents covering the environment. However, from swarm size 25 upwards, failures were almost exclusively of the stuck type. The C-shape at swarm size 35 had 196 stuck failures versus only 2 search failures — a near-complete absence of the search problem. This confirmed that the C-shape's failure mode is geometric: agents find the object and attach, but the concave geometry prevents the collective from generating a coordinated net force sufficient to move the payload to the target.

4.4 Rationale for Follow-Up Experiment

This failure mode analysis revealed two limitations in the main experiment's design that motivated a follow-up study. First, the SR sweep used only five coarse levels (0.0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0), which is insufficient to resolve whether an optimal noise level exists. This concern is directly supported by Bi et al. (PLOS Computational Biology), who demonstrate that optimal noise levels in collective transport are narrow and can be missed entirely by coarse parameter sweeps. Second, the shuffle duration was fixed at 2 seconds for all conditions. Wilson et al. (2014, Swarm Intelligence) argue that the duration of detachment-reattachment cycles is the primary lever for escaping force deadlocks, not merely the presence or absence of shuffling. At a fixed 2-second window, the simulation may never produce the emergent oscillatory dynamics that Gelblum et al. (PNAS, 2016) observe in real ant colonies navigating geometric obstacles, where informed and uninformed agents alternate in a way that effectively resolves deadlocks. Further, Brambilla et al. (Springer, 2022) show that for arbitrarily shaped objects, persistence time trades off against deadlock frequency in a shape-dependent manner, and a recent arXiv study (2024) on concave objects suggests that standard attachment strategies are fundamentally insufficient for this geometry class.

Based on this evidence, I designed a follow-up experiment targeting only the conditions where stuck failures dominate. The experiment uses L-shape and C-shape only, swarm sizes [25, 30, 35], an 11-level SR sweep [0.0, 0.1, 0.2, ..., 1.0] for finer resolution, and four shuffle durations [1s, 2s, 4s, 8s]. The time limit was increased from 180 seconds to 240 seconds: analysis of successful C-shape trials showed average completion times of 155–163 seconds, uncomfortably close to the old ceiling and likely causing right-censored failures that were not genuine stuck failures. With 50 trials per condition the follow-up totals 13,200 trials. The goal is to determine whether a combination of fine-grained SR level and longer shuffle duration can meaningfully recover success rates on geometrically challenging shapes, or whether the C-shape's concavity represents a hard limit for this transport strategy.
